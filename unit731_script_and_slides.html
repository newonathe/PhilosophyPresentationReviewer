Introduction ~25 seconds → Slide 1

Before our group examines the ethics of this case, I want to establish the facts — what happened, who was involved, what decisions were made, and what followed. pause The case we are presenting is Unit 731: a covert biological and chemical warfare research unit operated by the Imperial Japanese Army during World War II. It is one of the most extensively documented instances of state-sanctioned human experimentation in modern history — and it is our case for this course because its perpetrators were not soldiers in the conventional sense. They were trained scientists and physicians.

Origins & Establishment ~60 seconds → Slide 2

To understand how Unit 731 came to exist, we need to understand the context of 1930s Japan. Japan had been engaged in aggressive imperial expansion across East Asia, and its military leadership was acutely aware of Japan's industrial disadvantages relative to the Soviet Union and the United States. pause

In 1931, the Japanese Kwantung Army staged an explosion on a railway line in Manchuria — the so-called Mukden Incident — as a pretext to invade and occupy the territory. Manchuria gave Japan something strategically valuable: a vast, isolated region, far from international scrutiny, with a large and vulnerable civilian population.

pause

Into this context stepped Lt. Gen. Shirō Ishii — a trained microbiologist and military physician who had studied European biological warfare programs abroad. Ishii returned with a specific observation: the 1925 Geneva Protocol banned the use of biological weapons, but contained no mechanism for verification and no restriction on development. He argued to Army leadership that biological weapons were a militarily decisive and legally unregulated frontier. His proposal was approved. pause

A preliminary facility was established in Manchuria in 1932. After a prisoner revolt forced relocation, a purpose-built complex was constructed at Pingfang, south of Harbin. In 1936, Emperor Hirohito issued Imperial Decree No. 575 — formally authorizing what was publicly called the Epidemic Prevention and Water Purification Department of the Kwantung Army. That name was deliberate misdirection. The unit's actual mandate was the development of biological and chemical weapons through live human experimentation.

Key Moral Agents ~55 seconds → Slide 3

This case involves four distinct layers of moral agents — and my groupmates will analyze each one in depth. For now, I want to identify them factually.

pause

First, Ishii and the commanding officers who designed the program — trained professionals who understood exactly what the experiments would do to human bodies. Second, the research corps: over three thousand personnel drawn from Japan's top medical and scientific universities — many of whom actively sought posting to the unit for the research resources and career advancement it offered. Third, the Japanese state — which provided formal imperial authorization, concealed government funding, and the institutional cover of a public health mission.

pause

And fourth — the United States government who made a deliberate decision to give immunity to the Unit 731's commanders in exchange for biological warfare research data. Each of these agents made specific decisions. My groupmates will examine why those decisions were wrong. My task is to show you what those decisions were.

The Events ~80 seconds → Slides 4 & 5

The Pingfang complex at its peak employed over three thousand personnel and occupied roughly six square kilometers. It included laboratories, autopsy theaters, a private airstrip, a crematorium, and a prison block capable of holding approximately four hundred subjects at a time. pause

Subjects — referred to internally as maruta, the Japanese word for "logs" — were predominantly Chinese civilians and prisoners of war, alongside Soviet soldiers, Koreans, Mongolians, and in documented cases, Allied prisoners. The word "logs" was not casual language. It was an official administrative designation, used in documentation and internal records, designed to remove subjects from the moral consideration normally extended to persons.

pause

The experiments were systematic and lethal. Subjects were deliberately infected with plague, cholera, typhoid, and anthrax to measure infectious doses and transmission rates. Vivisection — surgery on conscious, living subjects, frequently without anesthesia — was performed to observe physiological responses in real time. Frostbite experiments involved immersing limbs in ice water until frozen. Pressure chamber experiments tested physical limits until blood vessels ruptured.

pause

And critically — Unit 731 was not only a laboratory program. In 1940, plague-infected fleas were dispersed by aircraft over Ningbo, China, triggering a civilian outbreak. In 1941, a similar operation targeted Changde, Hunan province. The program extended to a network of over sixty affiliated units across occupied China, Korea, and Southeast Asia — all operating under epidemic prevention cover.

Core Decisions ~55 seconds → Slide 5

I want to be precise about what the moral problem actually is — because that precision is what makes the ethical analysis that follows meaningful.

pause

The first core decision was Ishii's: to design a program of lethal human experimentation rather than pursue ethical alternatives. Defensive biological research — vaccines, disease prevention, field sanitation — was being pursued by other nations' military medical programs without using human subjects. That alternative existed and was known. The second was the Army's decision to authorize and sustain the program for over a decade with full institutional awareness of its nature. The third was the decision of individual researchers to participate — many voluntarily, some actively seeking the posting.

pause

And a fourth decision — the one that closes the case — was made by Ishii in August 1945, when Japan surrendered. He ordered the execution of surviving subjects, the burning of documents, and the partial demolition of the facility. That act of deliberate destruction demonstrates that those who ran the program knew precisely what it was.

Outcomes & Consequences ~65 seconds → Slide 6

The direct consequences: approximately three thousand deaths within the Pingfang complex. Broader estimates, including field biological weapons operations and affiliated units, range from tens of thousands to over three hundred thousand — figures that remain contested because the records were deliberately burned.

pause

What followed the war is equally significant. The Soviet Union conducted the Khabarovsk War Crimes Trials in 1949, convicting twelve captured Unit 731 personnel. Western governments dismissed the proceedings as Cold War propaganda — motivated partly by the fact that the United States had already made a different arrangement.

American biological warfare investigators had assessed Unit 731's data as militarily valuable. General MacArthur approved a secret immunity deal: full protection from prosecution in exchange for the research findings, which were transferred to the U.S. Army program at Camp Detrick, Maryland. pause

For context: in the same years, seven Nazi doctors were executed at Nuremberg for experiments of directly comparable character — hypothermia, pressure chambers, deliberate infection. The divergence in outcome is not explained by factual differences between the programs. It is explained by a deliberate U.S. strategic decision to prioritize intelligence value over accountability. Shirō Ishii died in 1959, never prosecuted. Many of his researchers returned to prominent careers in Japanese medicine and science.

Handoff to Part 2 ~20 seconds → Slide 7
Spoken transition

Those are the facts. pause My groupmate will now take us through the ethical analysis — examining what makes the core decisions I've just described not merely harmful, but specifically and philosophically wrong.

Origins & Establishment

Context

  • 1931: Japan occupies Manchuria — the Mukden IncidentThe invasion was triggered by a staged explosion on a railway line. Without this occupation, the scale and remoteness of the Pingfang complex would not have been achievable.

He pitched the program to Army leadership as both a defensive necessity and a strategic opportunity. His framing was strategic, not scientific.

The Experiments & the Maruta System

What Was Done
Suggested visual: Archival photograph of the Pingfang complex interior or exterior — widely available from the Unit 731 Museum and Wikimedia. Avoid graphic medical imagery; the slide's content carries the weight without it.
  • "Maruta" (logs) — the official administrative designation for human subjectsThis was not informal slang. It was an institutionalized practice embedded in documentation and verbal communication. The deliberate redesignation of persons as material objects was a structural mechanism — designed to remove subjects from the moral consideration normally extended to human beings, making participation feel professionally routine.
  • Deliberate infection: plague, cholera, anthrax, typhoid, dysentery, hemorrhagic feverSubjects were infected by injection, ingestion, and environmental exposure to determine infectious doses, incubation periods, and transmission rates for weapons development. Many underwent multiple infection cycles, kept alive between them to observe full disease progression.
  • Vivisection, frostbite, pressure chambers, weapons field testingSurgery on conscious subjects without anesthesia to observe live physiology; limb immersion in ice water until frozen; pressure chamber experiments causing physical rupture; subjects staked in open fields exposed to shells filled with biological and chemical agents. These were formal research protocols — not aberrations.
  • Victim population: primarily Chinese POWs and civilians, Soviet soldiers, Koreans, Mongolians, documented Allied prisonersVictims were defined by vulnerability — prisoner status, enemy nationality, geographic location in occupied territory — not by any action they had taken. Some victims were women and children. None consented. None were informed of what was being done to them in scientific terms.
Sources
Tsuneishi, K. (2005): Maruta classification system, experiment protocols, victim populations. apjjf.org
Gold, H. (2003): Unit 731 Testimony, Tuttle — first-person testimony on experimental procedures and institutional culture.
BBC News (2002): Accessible overview of experiment categories. bbc.com
Speaker note: The maruta system is the most important conceptual point on this slide — explain it fully in speech. Then move through the experiment categories quickly; listing two or three is enough. Don't dwell on graphic detail.

Scale, Network & Core Decisions

The Decision Points
Suggested visual: A simple map showing the network of affiliated units across East Asia, or a numbered 1–4 decision list graphic. Either anchors the audience spatially or structurally for this slide's content.
  • 60+ affiliated units across China, Korea, and Southeast Asia — all under epidemic prevention coverUnit 731 at Pingfang was the command center of a larger institutional network: Unit 1855 (Beijing), Unit 1644 (Nanjing), Unit 8604 (Guangzhou), and others extending into Korea, Malaysia, Singapore, and Thailand. The program's geographic scale demonstrates it was a state-level weapons program, not an isolated facility.
  • Field deployment: plague-infected fleas dispersed over Ningbo (1940) and Changde (1941)The transition from laboratory to field deployment confirms this was an active offensive weapons program. Civilian populations who never entered the Pingfang facility became victims of the biological warfare research conducted inside it. This dramatically expands the scope of the case beyond laboratory ethics.
  • Decision 1 — Ishii: human experimentation over ethical alternativesDefensive biological research — vaccine development, disease prevention, field sanitation — was in active use by other nations' military medical programs. The decision for lethal human experimentation was made for speed and directness, not necessity. The ethical path was available and known.
  • Decision 4 — August 1945: Ishii orders destruction of evidence and execution of surviving subjectsWhen Japan surrendered, Ishii ordered remaining subjects killed, documents burned, and facilities partially demolished. This act is morally significant in its own right: it demonstrates that those who ran the program understood it required concealment from the outside world. Awareness of culpability, expressed through destruction of evidence.

Outcomes & Consequences

What Followed
Suggested visual: A two-column comparison graphic — "Nuremberg (Nazi doctors): 7 executed" vs. "Unit 731 leadership: 0 prosecuted." This visual contrast is your single most powerful image on this slide and requires no photograph.
  • ~3,000 confirmed laboratory deaths; up to 300,000+ including field operations (contested)The 3,000 figure reflects documented deaths at Pingfang, based on post-war testimony and partial surviving records. Broader estimates incorporating field biological weapons deployments and affiliated units are significantly higher and remain historically debated — in part because the deliberate record destruction in 1945 made complete accounting impossible.
  • 1949 Khabarovsk Trials: 12 convicted by the USSR — dismissed as propaganda by the WestThe Soviet Union prosecuted 12 captured Unit 731 personnel. All were convicted and sentenced to labor camps. Western governments — the U.S. especially — publicly dismissed the proceedings as Cold War propaganda. The trial transcripts were, in fact, substantially accurate. The dismissal was motivated by the U.S. immunity deal already in place.
  • U.S. immunity deal: research data → Camp Detrick; zero prosecutions of leadership by Japan or U.S.MacArthur approved full immunity from prosecution in exchange for complete access to Unit 731's biological warfare research findings. The data went to the U.S. Army program at Fort Detrick. Ishii died in 1959, never prosecuted. Many researchers returned to prominent careers — including co-founding pharmaceutical companies still operating today.
  • Nuremberg comparison: 7 Nazi doctors executed for experiments of directly comparable characterHypothermia experiments, pressure chambers, deliberate infection — the same categories of experiment, the same professional identity of perpetrators, the same victim population type. The divergence in outcome is not explained by factual differences between the programs. It is explained by a deliberate U.S. decision to prioritize strategic intelligence value over criminal accountability.
Sources
U.S. National Archives: Declassified MacArthur immunity authorization and Hill-Victor Report. archives.gov

Nie, J. (2006): The Lancet — Nuremberg comparison and impact on medical ethics. thelancet.com

Speaker note: The Nuremberg comparison is your most powerful factual point — deliver it plainly. Let the contrast do the work.

Transition — From Facts to Analysis

Handoff
Suggested visual: Minimal — a clean slide with a single dividing line or the Nuremberg Code's first principle as a pull-quote: "The voluntary consent of the human subject is absolutely essential." (1947). This bridges your factual section to the ethical framework analysis that follows.
  • Summary line: what the facts establishTwo or three short recap lines — the program, its scale, the immunity outcome. Not a full list. A distilled reminder of what the audience has just heard, so they carry the factual picture into the analysis section.
  • Central question for Part 2: What makes these decisions specifically and philosophically wrong?State the analytical question explicitly as a bridge. The audience now has the facts. The next section provides the ethical vocabulary — Kant, utilitarianism, rights-based ethics, virtue ethics, natural law — to evaluate them. Framing this as a question signals intellectual structure and gives the next presenter a clean entry point.
Sources
No new citation. If using the Nuremberg Code quote, cite: Nuremberg Code (1947), Principle 1. Available via U.S. National Institutes of Health: history.nih.gov
Speaker note: Keep this slide on screen for 20–30 seconds maximum. Say your handoff line, make eye contact with your next groupmate, and yield the floor clearly. A confident, clean handoff reads as strong teamwork to the evaluator.
Final delivery notes
  • Do not read the slides. The bullets orient the audience; your script informs them. If you are reading the slide, the audience is reading it too — and nobody is listening to you.
  • The pause markers matter. A one-second pause after a key claim is not awkward — it is respectful of dense material. It also keeps your pace from accelerating under nerves.
  • Stay neutral until the handoff. Words like "horrific," "monstrous," or "unforgivable" belong to Part 2's language, not Part 1's. Neutral delivery of terrible facts is more powerful than editorializing.
  • If a question comes mid-presentation: Answer it in one or two sentences, then say "my groupmate will develop that further in the analysis section." Signals teamwork, keeps you on time.
